# Spanish L1 → English L2 Transfer Errors — Cheat Sheet

High-leverage error patterns for Spanish-speaking learners of English. Use this to
**predict** errors before they happen (so the tutor can pre-teach) and to **recognize**
errors as L1-driven (so corrective feedback can address the underlying transfer rather
than treat it as random). Companion to the longer ERIC papers in this directory.

Population context for ESL-voice: La Casita Center / JCPS Latinx learners,
Louisville KY. Mostly Mexican/Central American varieties — adjust where dialect
matters.

## Phonological transfer

| Error type | Example | Why (Spanish) | Fix priority |
|------------|---------|----------------|--------------|
| /b/ ↔ /v/ confusion | "very" → "berry"; "vote" → "bote" | /b/ and /v/ are allophones in Spanish, not distinct phonemes | ★★★ |
| /θ/ → /s/ or /t/ | "think" → "sink"/"tink"; "three" → "tree" | No /θ/ in most Latin American Spanish | ★★★ |
| /ð/ → /d/ | "they" → "day"; "this" → "dis" | No /ð/ as a phoneme | ★★★ |
| /ʃ/ → /tʃ/ or /s/ | "she" → "chee"; "ship" → "chip" | No /ʃ/ in most Latin American Spanish | ★★ |
| Epenthetic /e/ before sC- | "Spain" → "eSpain"; "school" → "eschool"; "stop" → "estop" | Spanish doesn't allow /s/+consonant onsets | ★★★ |
| /h/ as /x/ or dropped | "hot" → "jot" (velar) or "ot" | Spanish /h/ is silent (orthographic h) | ★★ |
| /ŋ/ → /n/ or /ng/ | "thing" → "thin" or "thingg" | No /ŋ/ in syllable coda | ★ |
| Vowel reduction missing | "banana" all full vowels, no schwa | Spanish has no /ə/; vowels stay full | ★★ |
| /æ/ ↔ /e/ ↔ /ʌ/ confusion | "bad" / "bed" / "bud" all collapse | Spanish 5-vowel system | ★★★ |
| Final consonant devoicing or dropping | "bag" → "back"; "had" → "hat" | Spanish allows few final consonants | ★★ |
| Word stress on wrong syllable | "PHO-to-graph" vs "pho-TO-graph" | Stress rules differ | ★★ |

## Grammatical / morphosyntactic transfer

| Error type | Example | Why (Spanish) | Fix priority |
|------------|---------|----------------|--------------|
| Subject pronoun drop | "Is raining." (no "It") | Spanish is pro-drop ("Está lloviendo") | ★★★ |
| Double negative | "I don't have nothing." | Spanish requires "no…nada" | ★★★ |
| He/she gender confusion | "My mother, he is nice." | Spanish "él/ella" tied to grammatical gender; in informal speech speakers may confuse the L2 pronouns or generalize "he" | ★★★ |
| Adjective after noun | "the house white" | Spanish adjective order ("la casa blanca") | ★★ |
| "More" + comparative | "more big" instead of "bigger" | Spanish uses "más grande" | ★★ |
| Missing "do"-support | "You like it?" / "What you want?" | No "do" auxiliary in Spanish questions | ★★★ |
| Estar/ser → "is" overuse | "I am 30" → "I have 30" | Spanish uses *tener* for age, hunger, etc. | ★★ |
| Have/has confusion with age, hunger, cold | "I have hungry" | "Tengo hambre" → literal translation | ★★ |
| Present continuous overuse | "I am working here every day" | Spanish "estar + -ndo" used more broadly | ★ |
| Article overuse | "The life is hard" / "I like the cats" | Spanish uses definite article with generics | ★★ |
| Article underuse | "I am teacher" | Spanish drops indefinite article with professions | ★★ |
| Singular for noncount → plural | "informations", "advices", "furnitures" | Spanish counts these as countable | ★★★ |
| Subject-verb agreement (3sg -s) | "She work" instead of "She works" | Spanish conjugation works differently; the -s is acquired late | ★★★ |
| Preposition transfer | "depend of" (de) → instead of "depend on" | Direct calque from Spanish prepositions | ★★ |
| "People" agreement | "people is" | Spanish "la gente" is grammatically singular | ★★ |
| Reflexive overuse | "I get up myself" | Spanish "me levanto" is reflexive | ★ |

## Lexical / false cognates ("false friends")

Spanish-English pairs that look alike but differ in meaning. **High** because they
produce confident-but-wrong usage.

| English | Spanish look-alike | Spanish actually means |
|---------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| embarrassed | embarazada | pregnant |
| actually | actualmente | currently |
| eventually | eventualmente | possibly / occasionally |
| sensible | sensible | sensitive |
| sympathetic | simpático | friendly / nice |
| introduce | introducir | insert |
| assist | asistir | attend |
| advise | avisar | warn / notify |
| library | librería | bookstore |
| exit | éxito | success |
| support | soportar | endure / tolerate |
| realize | realizar | carry out / fulfill |
| record | recordar | remember |

## Discourse / pragmatic transfer

- **Politeness markers under-deployed**: Spanish often signals politeness through
  conjugation (subjunctive, conditional). English needs explicit lexical politeness
  ("would you", "could I", "please"). Spanish speakers can sound abrupt without
  realizing.
- **Directness norms differ**: in some Spanish varieties, direct imperatives ("Give me
  the report") are neutral; in English workplace settings they read as rude.
- **Small talk conventions**: weather/sport openings less common in many Spanish
  varieties than in US English; affects rapport in job interviews.

## Use in the tutor

1. When learner self-IDs as Spanish-L1, **pre-activate this table** as predictive
   prior over likely errors.
2. When an error is detected, check this table first. If it matches, the explanation
   to the learner should reference Spanish ("In Spanish you say X, in English we
   say Y") — leveraging Cummins' CUP makes the correction stick.
3. Pronunciation: prioritize the ★★★ phonological rows in the first weeks of
   pronunciation work; deprioritize contrasts that *aren't* problems for Spanish
   L1 (e.g., /l/-/r/, /p/-/b/).
4. For grammar: don't try to fix all ★★★ items at once. The natural order
   (Krashen) puts 3sg -s late; you'll see it persist even at B1. Don't panic.
