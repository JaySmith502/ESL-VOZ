# ESL Pragmatics Inventory

The "knows grammar but can't actually ask for a refund" layer. Pragmatics covers
*how* language gets used in context — speech acts, register, politeness, indirectness.
Companion to `pragmatics_teaching_speech_acts_ncolctl.pdf` in this directory.

For a voice tutor this is critical: the learner can be grammatically correct and
still **socially wrong**, which is what blocks jobs, healthcare, and school
engagement.

---

## Speech acts to teach explicitly

Searle's five categories, with the moves an ESL learner needs to be functional.

### 1. Representatives (asserting, informing)
- Stating facts: "The store is closed."
- Describing: "It's a small green apartment."
- Reporting / narrating past events.
- Hedging certainty: "I think…", "I'm not sure but…", "Maybe…"

### 2. Directives (getting someone to do something) ★ critical for adult learners
- **Requests**: Could you / Would you mind / Can I have…?
- **Asking for clarification**: Sorry, could you repeat? / What does X mean?
- **Asking for help**: I need help with…
- **Giving instructions**: First… then… finally…
- **Refusing a request politely**: I'd love to but… / Unfortunately…

### 3. Commissives (committing to future action)
- Promising: I will / I'll definitely…
- Offering: Would you like…? / Can I help?
- Volunteering: I'll do it.
- Scheduling: How about Tuesday at 3?

### 4. Expressives (feelings / social moves) ★ rapport-building
- Greetings & openings: "How's it going?" / "How was your weekend?"
- Closings: "Take care" / "Talk to you soon"
- Apologizing: I'm sorry / I apologize for…
- Thanking + responding to thanks: You're welcome / No problem / Anytime
- Complimenting + responding: That's so nice of you / Thanks, I appreciate it
- Sympathizing: I'm sorry to hear that / That sounds hard

### 5. Declaratives (changing the world by speaking) — rarer in daily life
- Naming, pronouncing, voting, accepting/rejecting a contract.
- Mostly relevant for legal/civic/citizenship interactions.

## Register continuum

ESL learners often have one register (whatever they were exposed to). They need
to be able to **shift** based on context.

| Register | Marker examples | Where used |
|----------|------------------|-------------|
| Intimate  | nicknames, slang, in-jokes | family, close friends |
| Casual    | contractions, "yeah", "kinda" | friends, peers |
| Consultative | "could you", "I was wondering" | doctor, teacher, manager |
| Formal    | "I would like to", full sentences | job interview, court, written letter |
| Frozen    | scripted, ritual | citizenship oath, marriage vows |

**Voice tutor move:** name the register. "If you're at the doctor, we use the
*consultative* register — let's practice asking for help that way."

## Politeness strategies (Brown & Levinson, simplified)

The fundamental insight: **directness is rude in proportion to the imposition**.

Imposition continuum:
1. **Ask for the time** — bald imperative OK ("Excuse me, what time is it?")
2. **Ask to borrow a pen** — needs softening ("Could I borrow a pen real quick?")
3. **Ask for a day off** — needs hedging + reason ("I was wondering if it might be
   possible to take Friday off — my daughter has a school event.")
4. **Ask for a raise** — needs elaborate setup + relationship work.

**Politeness moves the tutor should teach:**
- **Hedging**: "I was wondering if…", "maybe", "kind of", "a little bit"
- **Negative politeness** (avoid imposing): "If it's not too much trouble…", "Whenever you have a moment…"
- **Positive politeness** (build rapport): "I really appreciate…", "You always…"
- **Indirect requests**: "Is there any chance…?" instead of "Give me…"
- **Pre-sequences**: "Are you busy right now?" before the actual ask.

## High-frequency situations the tutor should drill

For each situation: greet → state need → handle clarification → close.

### Healthcare
- "I have a pain in my…"
- "I need to make an appointment for…"
- "Could you explain that again? / What does that medication do?"
- "I don't understand. Can someone translate?"

### Workplace
- Calling out sick: "Hi, this is X. I'm not feeling well today, so I won't be in."
- Asking for time off: "I was wondering if I could take next Friday off."
- Clarifying tasks: "Just to make sure I understand — you want me to…?"
- Handling complaints / mistakes: "I'm sorry, that was my mistake. I'll fix it right away."

### School (for parents)
- Talking to teachers: "How is my child doing in math?"
- Reporting absences: "My child won't be in school tomorrow because…"
- Requesting a meeting / translator: "Could we have someone who speaks Spanish?"

### Civic / housing
- Calling a landlord about a repair: "The heat isn't working. It's been three days."
- Reporting an emergency: "I need help. There's a fire / someone is hurt / a car accident."
- DMV, social security, citizenship interviews: scripted Q&A — use USCIS 100q corpus.

### Banking / shopping
- Returning an item: "I'd like to return this. I have the receipt."
- Disputing a charge: "There's a charge on my account I don't recognize."
- Asking about fees: "What are the fees for…?"

## L1-driven pragmatic gaps for Spanish speakers

- Politeness is often signaled in Spanish through verb morphology (subjunctive,
  conditional). In English it has to be **lexicalized** ("could", "would", "maybe").
  Learners can sound abrupt because they've translated a polite Spanish form into a
  bare English imperative.
- Spanish small-talk norms differ from US norms — "how are you?" expects a real
  short answer, not just "fine".
- "Mande?" / "¿Cómo?" as clarification request → in English use "Sorry, what was that?"
  not "What?" (which can read as confrontational).

## Use in the runtime

1. Tag every dialog scenario with **speech act + register + imposition level**.
2. After a learner produces an utterance that's grammatical but mismatched on
   register or politeness, deliver the feedback **at the pragmatic level**, not the
   grammar level: "That's grammatically perfect — but at the doctor's office it
   sounds too direct. Try adding 'Could you' at the start."
3. For higher-stakes situations (job interview, citizenship), drill the **whole
   sequence** (greet → topic → close) rather than isolated turns.
